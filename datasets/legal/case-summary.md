# Waverly v. Redstone Mutual — Case Summary (Synthetic)

**Caption:** *Waverly v. Redstone Mutual Insurance Co.*

## Synopsis

The Fifteenth Circuit affirmed the district court's grant of summary judgment in favor of Redstone Mutual Insurance Co. in a coverage dispute under a commercial general liability ("CGL") policy. The court held that a pollution exclusion containing a **total pollution exclusion with a hostile fire exception** unambiguously barred coverage for claims arising from the release of refrigerant from a commercial HVAC unit, where the release did not result from a fire.

## Parties

- **Plaintiff-Appellant:** Nadia Waverly, individually and on behalf of Waverly Grocers, Inc.
- **Defendant-Appellee:** Redstone Mutual Insurance Co.

## Background

Waverly Grocers operates a chain of independent supermarkets in the Fifteenth Circuit. In 2023, refrigerant R-404A leaked from a walk-in freezer at Waverly's flagship location, causing evacuation of the store for three days and loss of approximately $220,000 in inventory. Customers and employees filed tort claims alleging respiratory injury from exposure to the refrigerant.

Waverly Grocers tendered the defense of those underlying claims to Redstone under its CGL policy. Redstone denied coverage, invoking the policy's pollution exclusion. Waverly Grocers brought a declaratory judgment action alleging breach of contract and bad-faith refusal to defend.

## Issue

Whether a release of refrigerant from an HVAC unit that does not involve fire falls within the policy's pollution exclusion, and if so, whether the "hostile fire" exception to that exclusion restores coverage.

## Holding

The pollution exclusion applies because (1) the refrigerant is a "pollutant" within the ordinary and plain meaning of the policy definition, which includes "gaseous irritants and contaminants," and (2) the hostile fire exception does not apply because no fire, hostile or otherwise, preceded or accompanied the release.

## Reasoning

The court applied the governing state law, which interprets insurance policies according to the ordinary meaning of the words used, construing ambiguities against the insurer. The court found no ambiguity. Citing *Amber Mills v. Colonial Casualty Co.* (5th Cir. 2019) (fictional) and *Parkside Eatery v. Evergreen Insurance* (15th Cir. 2022) (fictional), the panel held that gaseous refrigerants are pollutants for purposes of total pollution exclusions and that the hostile fire exception requires a fire as the proximate cause of the release.

The court rejected Waverly's reasonable-expectations argument, noting that the policy's definition of "pollutant" was set out in boldfaced, capitalized text in both the declarations page and the body of the policy, and that Waverly Grocers had been advised in writing of the scope of the exclusion at renewal. Accordingly, no reasonable insured could have expected coverage for the release at issue.

## Disposition

**Affirmed.** Costs on appeal are taxed against the appellant pursuant to Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 39.
